## 1. Capacity Utilization Factor (CUF / PLF)

### 1.1 Definition

Capacity Utilization Factor (CUF), also referred to as Plant Load Factor (PLF) in some regulatory and thermal-generation contexts, measures the ratio of actual energy generated by a solar power plant over a defined period to the theoretical maximum energy that plant could have generated if it had operated continuously at its rated capacity for the entire period.

CUF is a contractual and operational metric. It appears in Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs), MNRE performance benchmarks, and monthly O&M reports.

### 1.2 Standard Reference

IEC 61724-1:2021 defines the **Final Yield (Y_f)** as the normalised energy output per unit of installed power — the conceptual basis for CUF. Section 3 (Terms and Definitions) and Section 7 (Performance Indices) establish the normalisation framework on which CUF is built.

> **IEC 61724-1:2021, Section 7.3 — Final Yield (Y_f)**
> 
> Y_f = E_AC / P_o
> 
> Where E_AC is the net AC energy output (kWh) and P_o is the rated power of the PV array at STC (kWp).

**Note**: IEC 61724-1 strictly defines P_o as DC nameplate capacity. However, many PPAs (including SECI standard documents and CERC regulations) define CUF against contracted AC capacity. This calculator accepts both conventions.

CUF extends this by expressing the same ratio as a percentage of the theoretical maximum (rated capacity operating for all hours in the period):

### 1.3 Formula

```
CUF (%) = [ E_AC / (P_ref × T) ] × 100
```

### 1.4 Variable Definitions

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `E_AC` | Net AC energy delivered to the grid over the period | kWh |
| `P_ref` | Plant capacity reference (either DC or AC, depending on your reporting requirement) | kWp or kW |
| `T` | Total number of hours in the analysis period | hours |
| `CUF` | Capacity Utilization Factor | % |

**Note on T:** T is calculated as the number of calendar days in the analysis period multiplied by 24.

```
T = Days × 24
```

This is consistent with IEC 61724-1's use of calendar-based reference periods for annual and monthly performance reporting.

### 1.5 Capacity Convention

This calculator accepts both DC capacity (kWp) and AC capacity (kW) as valid inputs, because real-world solar projects use both conventions depending on the reporting requirement.

*DC capacity follows IEC 61724-1 and is used in O&M practice, feasibility studies, and MNRE reports

*AC capacity is required by CERC regulations and SECI standard PPAs, where CUF is defined against contracted AC capacity

The same plant produces different CUF values depending on which convention is used. Before reporting: Confirm which capacity base your PPA requires. This calculator accepts either — just label your report clearly.

### 1.6 Typical Operational Ranges

These ranges are indicative only. Actual CUF depends on irradiance, DC/AC ratio, tilt, tracking type, and maintenance quality.

| Plant Type | Typical Annual CUF Range |
|---|---|
| Fixed-tilt, moderate GHI (3.5–4.5 kWh/m²/day) | 16% – 20% |
| Fixed-tilt, high GHI (4.5–5.5 kWh/m²/day) | 19% – 23% |
| Single-axis tracker, high GHI | 22% – 27% |
| Dual-axis tracker, high GHI | 25% – 30% |

⚠️ Do not use these ranges for PPA compliance. Always compare against your specific plant's design CUF or PPA target. These ranges are for contextual awareness only.

### 1.7 Interpretation Notes

- CUF is a **summary metric**. A low CUF does not identify the cause of underperformance.
- CUF conflates resource variability (irradiance) with plant-side losses. A plant in a cloudy month can show low CUF with no equipment faults.
- CUF should always be reviewed alongside Performance Ratio (PR), irradiance data, and availability records.
- PLF in thermal generation is identical in formula but references AC installed capacity. When comparing solar CUF against a PLF target defined against AC capacity, the capacity reference must be confirmed before comparison. Even with matching capacity references, solar CUF (typically 15-25%) cannot be directly compared to thermal PLF (typically 70-90%) due to the fundamental difference in operating hours (daylight vs 24/7).

## 2. Degradation & Insolation Corrected CUF

### 2.1 Definition

Standard CUF does not account for module degradation or irradiance deviation from design-year assumptions.
Degradation & Insolation Corrected CUF normalizes for both factors, enabling fair comparison of actual generation against expected output from a degraded plant under actual irradiance conditions.

Note: This corrected calculation uses DC capacity only (kWp) . For conventional CUF (without corrections), this site's calculator accepts both DC and AC capacity to accommodate different PPA conventions.

### 2.2 Standard Reference

IEC 61724-1:2021, **Section 4 (Measurement Parameters)** and **Section 7 (Performance Indices)** establish the framework for normalised yield assessment. The degradation correction applies the module manufacturer's stated power degradation rate to the original nameplate capacity.

Module degradation rates are defined in product datasheets. Industry reference values for crystalline silicon modules:

- First-year degradation: 2%–3% (due to light-induced degradation, LID)
- Subsequent annual degradation: 0.4%–0.7% per year (IEC 61215 long-term characterisation studies; NREL Photovoltaic Degradation Rates — Jordan & Kurtz, *Progress in Photovoltaics*, 2013)

### 2.3 Formula

**Step 1 — Degradation-Adjusted DC Capacity**

```
P_DC_corrected = P_DC_nameplate × [ 1 - (D1/100) ] × [ 1 - (Da/100) ]^(n-1)
```

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `P_DC_nameplate` | Original nameplate DC capacity at commissioning | kWp |
| `D1` | First-year degradation rate | % |
| `Da` | Annual degradation rate (year 2 onwards) | % |
| `n` | Number of years since commissioning (n ≥  0.01) | years |
| `P_DC_corrected` | Degradation-adjusted DC capacity | kWp |

**Step 2 — Insolation Scaling Factor**

```
SF = H_POA_actual / H_POA_design
```

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `H_POA_actual` | Measured POA insolation in the reporting period | kWh/m² |
| `H_POA_design` | Design-year POA insolation for the same period | kWh/m² |
| `SF` | Insolation Scaling Factor | dimensionless |

**Step 3 — Corrected CUF**

```
CUF_corrected (%) = [ E_AC / (P_DC_corrected × SF × T) ] × 100
```

### 2.4 When This Matters

Standard CUF becomes increasingly misleading after year 3–4 of plant operation, when accumulated degradation exceeds ~1.5%. Comparing current-year CUF against the original design-year CUF target without degradation correction will show apparent underperformance that is simply expected aging, not a real fault.

## 3. Specific Yield

### 3.1 Definition

Specific Yield (also called **Final Yield** in IEC 61724-1) is the net AC energy output of the plant per unit of installed DC capacity over a defined period. It normalises generation by plant size, enabling direct comparison between plants of different installed capacities operating at the same site under the same irradiance conditions.

### 3.2 Standard Reference

**IEC 61724-1:2021, Section 7.3 — Final Yield (Y_f)**

> Y_f = E_AC / P_o

Where:
- `E_AC` = net AC energy output (kWh)
- `P_o` = rated power of the PV array at STC (kWp)

The unit is **kWh/kWp**, or when expressed per day: **kWh/kWp/day**.

### 3.3 Formula

**Total Specific Yield over the period:**

```
Y_f = E_AC / P_DC
```

**Daily Specific Yield (daily average over the period):**

```
Y_f_daily = E_AC / (P_DC × N)
```

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `E_AC` | Net AC energy output over the period | kWh |
| `P_DC` | Installed DC capacity at STC | kWp |
| `N` | Number of days in the analysis period | days |
| `Y_f` | Specific Yield (total period) | kWh/kWp |
| `Y_f_daily` | Daily average Specific Yield | kWh/kWp/day |

### 3.4 Interpretation Notes

- Specific Yield reflects both the solar resource and overall system losses. It does not isolate plant-side efficiency independently of irradiance.
- A higher Specific Yield at a given site, compared between two plants, indicates better resource capture or lower system losses — but the irradiance conditions must be confirmed to be equivalent before concluding a difference in plant performance.
- Typical daily Specific Yield values for ground-mount fixed-tilt plants in India: **3.2–5.0 kWh/kWp/day** depending on region and season.
- IEC 61724-1 Table 1 classifies Y_f as a **system performance index** (not a loss parameter).

## 4. Reference Yield

### 4.1 Definition

Reference Yield represents the solar energy available to the array per unit area, expressed as equivalent peak sun hours — the number of hours at standard test condition irradiance (1 kW/m²) that would deliver the measured plane-of-array insolation.

### 4.2 Standard Reference

**IEC 61724-1:2021, Section 7.1 — Reference Yield (Y_r)**

> Y_r = H_i / G_STC

Where:
- `H_i` = plane-of-array (POA) insolation (kWh/m²)
- `G_STC` = irradiance at standard test conditions = **1 kW/m²**

### 4.3 Formula

```
Y_r = H_POA / G_STC
```

**Daily average Reference Yield:**

```
Y_r_daily = (H_POA / G_STC) / N
```

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `H_POA` | Measured plane-of-array insolation over the period | kWh/m² |
| `G_STC` | Standard test condition irradiance = 1 kW/m² | kW/m² |
| `N` | Number of days in the analysis period | days |
| `Y_r` | Reference Yield (total period) | hours (equivalent peak sun hours) |
| `Y_r_daily` | Daily average Reference Yield | hours/day |

### 4.4 Interpretation Notes

- Reference Yield is a **resource parameter**, not a performance parameter. It characterises what the sun provided, not what the system did with it.
- Y_r is the denominator in the Performance Ratio calculation (IEC 61724-1, Section 7.5).
- POA irradiance must be measured at the actual array tilt and azimuth, not on a horizontal plane. Using GHI in place of POA insolation introduces a systematic error that varies seasonally. See IEC 61724-1, Section 5 (Instrumentation).

## 5. Performance Target Index (PTI)

### 5.1 Definition

Performance Target Index (PTI) is an operational KPI used to compare actual Specific Yield against the expected Specific Yield under the same irradiance conditions, given an anticipated Performance Ratio.

### 5.2 Standard Reference

**PTI is not defined in IEC 61724-1.** It is a custom comparative metric used in operational O&M reporting to provide a quick dimensionless flag for over- or under-performance relative to a PR assumption.

This distinction is disclosed on the calculator page and is documented here for completeness.

### 5.3 Formula

```
PTI = Y_f_daily / (Y_r_daily × PR_expected)
```
**Note on input:** This calculator accepts PR_expected as a percentage (e.g., 55.78 for 55.78%). The value is internally converted to decimal before applying the formula shown above.

| Variable | Description | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `Y_f_daily` | Actual daily Specific Yield | kWh/kWp/day |
| `Y_r_daily` | Daily Reference Yield (peak sun hours/day) | hours/day |
| `PR_expected` | Anticipated Performance Ratio for the plant | % (entered as percentage, converted internally) |
| `PTI` | Performance Target Index | dimensionless |

### 5.4 Interpretation Ranges

| PTI Range | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 0.80 | Underperformance — actual yield is below what the irradiance and expected PR should produce |
| 0.80 – 1.20 | Normal operating range |
| > 1.20 | Overperformance or unrealistic inputs — verify PR assumption and irradiance data |

### 5.5 Limitations

- PTI result is entirely dependent on the accuracy of the `PR_expected` input. An optimistic PR assumption will produce a PTI that flags underperformance on a correctly functioning plant.
- PTI should not be used for PPA compliance reporting or contractual purposes. It is an internal diagnostic flag only.
- For formal performance evaluation, use IEC 61724-1 Performance Ratio (Y_f / Y_r) directly.

## 6. Capacity-Based Performance Ratio (PR)

### 6.1 Definition

The Capacity-Based Performance Ratio (PR) compares a solar plant's actual energy output to a reference energy yield derived from measured plane-of-array irradiance. It is normalized using the installed DC capacity and the STC reference irradiance of 1 kW/m², as defined in IEC 61724-1. It is the standard and the most widely used performance index in utility-scale and C&I solar O&M practice.

### 6.2 Standard Reference

**IEC 61724-1:2021, Section 7.5 — Performance Ratio (PR)**

> PR = Y_f / Y_r

Where:
- `Y_f` = Final Yield = E_AC / P_o (kWh/kWp)
- `Y_r` = Reference Yield = H_i / G_STC (hours)
- `P_o` = rated DC power at STC (kWp)
- `G_STC` = 1 kW/m² (fixed constant, non-adjustable)

Expanding:

```
PR = [ E_AC / P_DC ] / [ H_POA / G_STC ]
```

Which simplifies to:

```
PR (%) = [ E_AC / (P_DC × H_POA / G_STC) ] × 100
```

### 6.3 Inputs

| Input | Description | Unit | Validation |
|-------|-------------|------|------------|
| Energy Generated | Net AC energy output during the period | kWh | ≥ 0.01 |
| Plant DC Capacity | Total nameplate DC capacity of installed PV modules at STC | kWp | ≥ 0.01 |
| POA Insolation | Measured plane-of-array insolation during the same period | kWh/m² | ≥ 0.01 |
| Irradiance @ STC | Standard test condition irradiance reference | kW/m² | Fixed at 1 — not user-adjustable |

### 6.4 Output Flags

| Result | Flag |
|--------|------|
| PR > 96% | ⚠️ "Suspiciously High" — verify input data quality |
| PR > 100% | ❌ "Statistical Error" — result is physically impossible; check inputs |

**Why 96% is the suspicion threshold:** Real-world capacity-based PR values above 95–96% are extremely rare under normal operating conditions. Values in this range almost always indicate a data quality issue — misaligned irradiance sensor, incorrect DC capacity entry, or time-alignment errors between energy and irradiance records.

### 6.5 Implementation Notes

- `G_STC` is fixed at **1 kW/m²** and is not user-adjustable. This is consistent with IEC 61724-1:2021, which defines G_STC as a fixed physical constant.
- This calculator uses **DC nameplate capacity** as the normalisation reference, following IEC 61724-1. Using AC capacity instead would conflate inverter sizing decisions with module-level generation performance.
- POA insolation must be measured at the same tilt and azimuth as the PV array. Using GHI instead of POA will produce systematically incorrect PR values — typically 5–15% lower depending on tilt and latitude.

### 6.6 Worked Example

| Input | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Energy Generated | 450 kWh |
| Plant DC Capacity | 100 kWp |
| POA Insolation | 5.5 kWh/m² |
| Irradiance @ STC | 1.0 kW/m² (fixed) |

```
Y_f = 450 / 100 = 4.5 kWh/kWp
Y_r = 5.5 / 1.0 = 5.5 h
PR = (4.5 / 5.5) × 100 = 81.8%
```

**Result: 81.8%**

### 6.7 Limitations

- Capacity-based PR does not correct for temperature effects. In high-temperature periods, PR will decrease even when the plant is operating correctly. Use [Temperature-Corrected PR](#9-temperature-corrected-performance-ratio-pr) for temperature-normalised analysis.
- PR is highly sensitive to POA sensor accuracy. Sensor soiling, misalignment, or calibration drift directly shifts the PR result.
- In DC-oversized plants, inverter clipping during peak irradiance reduces AC output while POA continues to accumulate, which will suppress PR even when the plant is performing as designed.
- Capacity-based PR is most reliable for trend analysis within the same plant over consistent periods. Cross-plant PR comparisons require confirmation that capacity conventions, sensor placement, and time-alignment methods are identical.

## 7. Efficiency-Based Performance Ratio (PR)

### 7.1 Definition

Efficiency-Based Performance Ratio (PR) compares a solar plant's actual energy output to a reference energy yield calculated from measured plane-of-array irradiance, the total active module area, and the module reference efficiency.

### 7.2 Standard Reference

The standard International Electrotechnical Commission IEC 61724-1 defines PR as the ratio of Final Yield to Reference Yield

> PR = Y_f / Y_r

If the reference power is algebraically expressed as

```
P_eff = A_total × η_module × G_STC
```

Where `P_eff` is the effective reference power (kW) at STC. The PR is then:

```
PR (%) = [ E_AC / (P_eff × H_POA / G_STC) ] × 100
```

Expanding:

```
PR (%) = [ E_AC / (A_total × η_module × H_POA) ] × 100
```

### 7.3 Inputs

| Input | Description | Unit | Validation |
|-------|-------------|------|------------|
| Energy Generated | Net AC energy output during the period | kWh | ≥ 0.01 |
| POA Insolation | Measured plane-of-array insolation during the same period | kWh/m² | ≥ 0.01 |
| Total Active Module Area | Total active surface area of all PV modules installed in the plant. Excludes land area, walkways, spacing, and non-generating surfaces | m² | ≥ 0.01 |
| Module Efficiency | Rated module conversion efficiency from manufacturer datasheet | % | 10% – 40% |

**On Module Efficiency range:** The validation range of 10–40% reflects the practical boundaries of commercially available PV technology. Standard monocrystalline silicon modules typically fall between 19–23%. Thin-film modules range from approximately 10–18%. Values outside 10–40% are rejected as likely input errors.

### 7.4 Output Flags

Same thresholds as Capacity-Based PR:

| Result | Flag |
|--------|------|
| PR > 96% | ⚠️ "Suspiciously High" — verify input data quality |
| PR > 100% | ❌ "Statistical Error" — result is physically impossible; check inputs |

### 7.5 Limitations

- Efficiency-based PR is sensitive to the accuracy of the module efficiency input. Using a datasheet efficiency value that does not reflect actual in-field module condition (post-degradation) will overstate the reference power and understate PR.
- Like capacity-based PR, this method does not correct for temperature effects.
- Total Active Module Area must accurately represent the generating surface only. Including non-active area (frame borders, spacing) will inflate the area input and suppress the PR result.

## Notes on Implementation

- All calculations execute entirely client-side in the user's browser (JavaScript).
- No plant data, energy figures, or inputs of any kind are transmitted to any server.
- Input validation is applied on all fields. Results outside physically plausible ranges trigger a warning rather than silent acceptance.
- Where inputs are estimated (for example, back-calculated POA insolation from plant output in the Solar Insolation calculator), this is stated explicitly in the calculator interface and should be treated as directional, not auditable.
